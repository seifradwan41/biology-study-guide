Biology study guides: Skin and integumentary system, circulatory system, blood and lymphatic system, and muscular system — notes and Q&A

What is the integumentary system?

Skin + hair + nails. The skin is the body's largest organ.

5 Functions

Protection Body temp regulation Excretion Information gathering Vitamin D production
  • Protection: blocks pathogens, UV radiation, and water loss
  • Temperature: releases excess heat, retains some body heat
  • Excretion: sweat glands release salts and urea
  • Info gathering: sensory receptors for pressure, pain, heat, cold
  • Vitamin D: skin uses sunlight to produce it (NOT vitamin B)

3 Layers of Skin

LayerLocationKey Features
EpidermisOuter (what you see)Upper layer = dead cells. Inner layer = stem cells dividing rapidly. Contains keratin (tough fibrous protein) and melanocytes (make melanin — brown pigment that absorbs UV)
DermisMiddle / inner thick layerContains blood vessels, nerves, sebaceous glands, sweat glands, hair follicles, muscles. Regulates body temp via blood vessel dilation/constriction.
HypodermisDeepest layerFat + connective tissue. Insulates the body.

Key Structures & Their Jobs

  • Sebaceous glands → secrete sebum (oily substance that keeps epidermis flexible, waterproof, and kills bacteria)
  • Sweat glands → excrete salts and urea; sweat evaporates → cools body
  • Hair follicles → pockets in epidermis extending into dermis; rapid cell growth at base causes hair to grow
  • Nails → nail root = rapidly dividing cells at fingertips/toes that fill with keratin
  • Blood vessels in dermis → narrow when body needs to conserve heat; widen when body needs to release heat
Both hair and nails are made of keratin — the same protein made by skin cells.

Hair Functions by Location

  • Head hair → protects scalp from sun
  • Nose hair → keeps dust out of lungs
  • Ear hair → keeps sand/particles out
  • Eyelashes → keep dirt and bugs out of eyes
  • Body hair → provides insulation from cold

How does the dermis help regulate body temperature?
Blood vessels in dermis narrow to conserve heat; they widen to release heat from the body's core to the skin.
How does sweat keep you cool?
When sweat evaporates, it takes heat away from your body.

Function of the Circulatory System

Transports oxygen, nutrients, and other substances throughout the body. Removes wastes from tissues.

The Heart

  • Myocardium = the muscle layer of the heart; its contractions pump blood
  • The heart has 4 chambers: right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, left ventricle
  • Septum = wall that separates the right side from the left side
  • Atrium (upper) = receives blood coming in from the body
  • Ventricle (lower) = pumps blood out of the heart
  • Valves = flaps of connective tissue between atria/ventricles and between ventricles/blood vessels; keep blood moving in ONE direction

2 Circulation Pathways

PathwaySide of HeartDestinationBlood Change
PulmonaryRight sideLungsOxygen-poor → Oxygen-rich (picks up O₂, releases CO₂)
SystemicLeft sideRest of bodyOxygen-rich → Oxygen-poor (cells absorb O₂, load CO₂)
The left side is larger and more muscular than the right — it pumps blood through the entire body, not just the lungs.

How the Heart Beats (Electrical System)

SA node fires → impulse spreads through atria → atria contract → AV node picks up impulse → impulse delayed (atria pump blood to ventricles) → AV node spreads impulse through ventricles → ventricles contract → blood pumped out

  • SA node (sinoatrial node) = pacemaker; triggers atria to contract
  • AV node (atrioventricular node) = triggers ventricles to contract
  • Nervous system influences SA node → increases or decreases heart rate

3 Types of Blood Vessels

VesselStructureFunction
ArteriesThick, elastic walls; connective tissue + smooth muscleCarry blood away from the heart to body tissues. All carry oxygen-rich blood EXCEPT pulmonary arteries.
CapillariesSmallest vessels; extremely thin wallsO₂ and nutrients diffuse into tissues; wastes move into blood. Site of actual exchange.
VeinsLarge vessels; many have valves to prevent backflowCarry blood toward the heart. Return blood from body to heart.

Blood Pressure

  • = wave of fluid pressure in arteries created by heart contractions
  • Regulated by the brain and kidneys
  • When BP falls → nervous system → neurotransmitters relax smooth muscles in vessel walls
  • When BP rises → nervous system → neurotransmitters cause smooth muscles to contract
Why do large multicellular animals need a circulatory system while simple animals don't?
Simple animals have cells in direct contact with the environment — diffusion handles everything. Large organisms have cells far from the environment, so they need a system to deliver O₂/nutrients and remove wastes.
City analogy: if streets = circulatory system, what does a city represent?
The body. Streets/highways = blood vessels, delivery people = blood cells, supplies (food) = O₂ and nutrients, garbage = CO₂ and waste products.
Pulmonary circulation: which side of heart, where does blood go, what blood change?
Right side → Lungs → Oxygen-poor becomes oxygen-rich
Systemic circulation: which side of heart, where does blood go, what blood change?
Left side → Body → Oxygen-rich becomes oxygen-poor
What is the SA node and why is it called the pacemaker?
A small group of cardiac muscle fibers (sinoatrial node) that fires electrical impulses to start each heartbeat by triggering atria to contract.
What does the AV node do?
Picks up the impulse from the SA node, delays it while the atria pump blood to the ventricles, then spreads impulses through the ventricles causing them to contract.
Where does oxygen-rich blood from the lungs enter the heart?
Left atrium
What part of the heart pumps oxygen-poor blood to the lungs?
Right ventricle
Which vessel returns blood from the head and arms to the heart?
Superior vena cava
Which pulmonary blood vessel carries oxygen-rich blood?
Pulmonary vein (remember: veins return to heart; by the time blood is returning from the lungs it's oxygen-rich)
Why are artery walls thicker than vein walls?
Arteries carry blood directly from the heart under high pressure — they need to be stronger and more elastic to withstand the pumping force.
Why is the left side of the heart larger and more muscular than the right?
The left side pumps blood through the entire body (systemic); the right side only pumps to the lungs. More distance = more force needed.

Blood — 4 Components

ComponentCharacteristicsFunction
PlasmaStraw-colored fluid; ~90% water + dissolved gases, salts, nutrients, enzymes, hormones, waste products, plasma proteins (albumin, globulins, fibrinogen), cholesterolControls body temp, transports substances (fatty acids, hormones, vitamins), helps with osmotic pressure and blood clotting
Red blood cellsDisk-shaped, thinner in center. Contain hemoglobin (iron-containing protein) — gives blood its red colorHemoglobin binds O₂ in lungs and releases it in capillaries. Transport O₂ to cells and some CO₂ to lungs.
White blood cellsTypes: macrophages, B lymphocytes, T lymphocytesMacrophages engulf pathogens. B lymphocytes produce antibodies. T lymphocytes fight tumors and viruses. Overall: guard against infection.
PlateletsFragments of cytoplasm of bone marrow cells; enclosed in a membraneCluster around wounds → release clotting factors → form fibrin (sticky filaments) → clot that stops bleeding
Human body contains 4–6 liters of blood (NOT 8–10). Plasma is ~90% water (NOT 50%). Albumin/globulins/fibrinogen are proteins (NOT nucleic acids).

Clotting Steps

Injury occurs → platelets clump at injury site → release proteins → formation of fibrin (sticky filaments) → fibrin mesh forms a clot → bleeding stops.

The Lymphatic System

  • Network of vessels, nodes, and organs that collects fluid leaking out of capillaries, screens it for microorganisms, and returns it to the circulatory system
  • Lymph = fluid that consists of blood components that moved through capillary walls
  • Lymph nodes = filter nodes that trap microorganisms, stray cancer cells, and debris; white blood cells there destroy cellular trash. If many microorganisms are trapped → nodes become swollen
  • Lymph is moved by pressure from skeletal muscles (NOT by the heart)
  • Valves in lymph vessels prevent backflow
  • Lymph returns to bloodstream through veins just below the shoulders
  • Picks up fats and fat-soluble vitamins from digestive tract
  • Spleen = lymphatic organ that stores platelets
  • Thymus = also a lymphatic organ

3 Circulatory System Diseases

DiseaseWhat HappensKey Notes
Heart disease / AtherosclerosisFatty deposits (plaques) build up in artery walls → arteries stiffenCan cause angina (chest pain from restricted blood flow), heart failure (heart damaged by O₂ deprivation), heart attack (plaque cap ruptures + clot blocks artery → heart muscle tissue damaged/dies)
StrokeClot blocks blood vessel in brain → brain cells die suddenlyCan also occur if a weak vessel breaks (bleeding in brain). Same mechanism as heart attack but in the brain.
Hypertension (high BP)BP consistently above 140/90Damages heart and blood vessels. Causes small tears in vessels where plaques form. Leads to heart attack, stroke, kidney damage.

Cholesterol & Circulatory Disease

  • Cholesterol = lipid that is part of animal cell membranes
  • Transported by LDL (low-density lipoprotein) and HDL (high-density lipoprotein)
  • Made in the liver; also comes from foods high in fat
  • Normal cholesterol range: 100–200 mg/dL
  • Normal liver cells: LDL binds receptors → taken into cell → stored/used. If cholesterol levels are high → liver stops producing more. ✓
  • Defective liver cells: LDL cannot bind receptors → cholesterol stays in blood + liver keeps producing more → excess cholesterol deposits in arteries → atherosclerosis ✗
  • High-fat diets → liver cells store extra cholesterol → stop making LDL receptors → cholesterol not removed → deposited in arteries
  • Prevention: low-fat diet prevents LDL from rising; exercise keeps heart muscles strong

Chest pain caused by restricted blood flow to the heart.

Blood flow to heart muscle tissue is blocked → tissue is damaged/dies. Usually caused when a plaque cap ruptures and a blood clot completely blocks an artery.

Muscle Contraction — How it Works

  • Skeletal muscle fibers are packed with myofibrils — bundles of protein filaments
  • Myosin = thick filaments | Actin = thin filaments
  • Actin filaments are bound at areas called Z lines
  • Sarcomere = two Z lines + the filaments between them (basic unit of contraction)
  • During contraction: myosin (powered by ATP) forms cross-bridges with actin → pulls actin toward center of sarcomere → sarcomere shortens → muscle contracts
  • Motor neurons release calcium ions inside muscle fibers to trigger contraction
  • Motor neuron meets muscle fiber at a synapse = neuromuscular junction
  • Neurotransmitter = acetylcholine (carries nerve impulse across synapse to muscle)
  • Stronger contraction = more muscle fibers contracting (not harder contraction per fiber)
Memory trick: Actin is shorter word = thinner filament. Myosin is longer word = thicker filament.

Muscles and Movement

  • Tendons = strips of connective tissue that attach skeletal muscles to bones
  • Bones act as levers, joints act as fulcrums
  • Muscles work in opposing pairs — when one contracts, the other relaxes
  • Biceps contracts → elbow bends (arm curls up)
  • Triceps contracts → elbow straightens (arm extends)

Chain of Events That Produce Motion

Thick (myosin) & thin (actin) filaments slide past each other
↓
Muscle fibers shorten
↓
Muscle contracts
↓
Muscle pulls on tendon
↓
Tendon pulls on bone
↓
Body part moves
Without tendons → we would NOT be able to move. Each effect becomes the cause of the next event in the chain.
